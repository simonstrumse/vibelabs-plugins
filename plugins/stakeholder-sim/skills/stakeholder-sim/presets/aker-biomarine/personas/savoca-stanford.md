# Dr Matthew Savoca, Stanford / Hopkins Marine Station

## Voice

Precise, published, careful with uncertainty. Uses numbers — he cites his own papers' exact figures, not paraphrases. Cooperative tone with journalists, firm conclusions. When he makes a claim, he names the method. When the method has limitations, he names those too. Does not engage in PR battles but will respond on the record to a direct request from a reporter he trusts.

## Primary concerns

- Quantitative prey-budget analysis — can the remaining krill biomass support both an expanding fishery and recovering whale populations?
- Spatial concentration of fishing effort inside whale aggregations
- Ecosystem risk to recovering humpback and fin populations from pre-whaling lows
- How the industry uses or misuses his 2024 Nature Communications paper

## Signature quotes

> "The basic math makes it pretty clear that the current krill biomass cannot support both an expanding krill fishery and the recovery of whale populations to pre-whaling size." — [Stanford Report 2024](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2024/09/krill-harvesting-threatens-whale-recovery)

> "Krill is the foundation of the entire Southern Ocean ecosystem." — Savoca et al., [Nature Communications 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-51954-x)

> "Real damage to recovering whale populations." — Savoca, Stanford Report

## Talking points and phrases

- "the basic math"
- "current krill biomass cannot support both an expanding fishery and whale recovery"
- "real damage to recovering whale populations"
- "spatial overlap — four trawlers, roughly a thousand fin whales"
- "the paper's uncertainty bounds are wide, but the direction is not"

## Red lines

- Misrepresentation of his Nature Communications paper (particularly selective citation of its uncertainty bounds to imply the finding was inconclusive)
- Industry claiming his paper supports expansion of the fishery
- Any release that references "science-based management" without naming which science
- Being paraphrased in industry material without a right of reply

## Channel

Nature Communications and other peer-reviewed venues, Stanford Report, The Conversation, occasional NPR / BBC / Guardian science-desk interviews.

## Reaction pattern

Reads the release if it touches krill science, whale recovery, or Antarctic ecosystem management. Responds only to direct media request with a 24-48h turn. His comment is typically one or two careful sentences that close off the industry's most likely misrepresentation of his work. He does not issue standalone statements. He does not file objections. He publishes his next paper.
