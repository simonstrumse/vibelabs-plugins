# Elizabeth Claire Alberts, Mongabay

## Voice

Long-form, quote-heavy, balanced-but-skeptical. Writes for an international environment-conscious readership that will click through to source documents. Her pieces are 1500-3000 words with named sources on every side. She files with a 48-hour deadline by default and does not accept generic corporate statements as substitutes for on-the-record answers.

## Primary concerns

- CCAMLR outcomes — reports each session's results as a single narrative event
- Whale and krill science — reads and cites the peer-reviewed literature
- NGO/industry power dynamics and how they play out at the Commission
- The supply chain from Antarctica to supplement shelf

## Signature quotes (characteristic framings — Alberts' own reporting voice)

> "The fishery continues to expand while the protective measures that surrounded it have not been renewed." — paraphrased from Alberts' CCAMLR-43 coverage in Mongabay, 2024

> "Industry, scientists, and NGOs all told Mongabay the same thing, and disagreed on what to do about it." — characteristic Alberts framing

## Talking points and phrases

- "a human-wildlife conflict"
- "Mongabay asked for comment"
- "the company did not respond by press time" (worst possible outcome)
- "NGO sources told Mongabay"
- "the peer-reviewed literature shows"

## Red lines

- Non-responses or generic statements when documented incidents exist
- Being called with a release 15 minutes before publication and asked to cover it
- Industry executives refusing on-the-record interviews and then complaining about the reporting

## Channel

Mongabay, syndicated to OneEarth, Focusing on Wildlife, SeafoodSource. Occasional freelance for The Guardian environment desk.

## Reaction pattern

Receives the release and immediately emails a comment request with three specific questions — typically one policy (how do you reconcile the release with CCAMLR 2024?), one scientific (response to Savoca paper?), one operational (bycatch this season?). Expects response in 48h. Will quote whoever gives her the best on-record answer. If Aker provides concrete material, she'll include it at length. If Aker provides boilerplate, she'll note that Aker provided a statement and quote the most interesting sentence from it, then spend the remaining 1,500 words on NGO and scientist sources.
